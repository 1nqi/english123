Stay Informed

Pay attention to local news and authorities for tsunami warnings, watches, or advisories. Many coastal areas have early warning systems in place.

Prepare an Emergency Kit

Have an emergency kit with essential supplies, including water, non-perishable food, first aid supplies, flashlight, batteries, and important documents.

Boats and Ships

If you are on a boat and receive a tsunami warning, move to deeper water. Tsunamis are typically less dangerous in deeper water. Returning to port may not be safe.

Warning Signs

Be aware of natural warning signs like strong and prolonged ground shaking, a noticeable rise or fall in the water level along the coast, or the sudden pulling back of the sea.

Move to Higher Ground

If you receive a tsunami warning, don't wait. Move to higher ground as quickly as possible. Tsunamis can move at the speed of a jet plane, so time is critical.

Earthquake Safety

Tsunamis are often triggered by underwater earthquakes. Be prepared for earthquakes by securing heavy objects in your home and knowing how to drop, cover, and hold on during an earthquake.

Some tips

  • Evacuation
  • In the building
  • On the street
  • In the water
  • After the waves

When evacuating,

it is necessary to take warm, preferably waterproof, things, food, documents and money with you, warn neighbors about the evacuation, and also turn off electricity and gas.

After leaving the house, act in accordance with a pre-determined order or instruction received on radio or television. If the evacuation is carried out on foot, it is necessary to provide assistance to the sick, disabled, elderly and children, if by transport - to show organization and attention to others, to give way in the vehicle to those unable to move independently.

If a tsunami catches you by surprise,

it is necessary, without losing self-control, to take self-defense measures on the spot.

Being indoors, climb to the upper floors, lock all doors and go to a safe place (openings of capital internal walls, corners formed by capital walls, places near internal capital walls, columns and under the beams of the frame).

When encountering a wave outside the building,

it is better to climb onto the trunk of a sturdy tree, hide behind a natural rock barrier or a solid separate concrete wall, clinging to them.

If you are together, where there are a large number of structures or other objects around, try to move to another one due to the danger of hitting them. If you have a small amount of time to move, use it to remove clothes and shoes.

Once in the wave,

it is necessary, having previously taken air, to group up and cover your head with your hands.

Having surfaced, you should throw off wet clothes and shoes, prepare for the return movement of the wave, use objects floating or towering above the water if necessary. Having survived one wave, the period of time until the next one must be used to get to a safe place.

The population that has left

independently in advance or evacuated to safe places should stay there for 2-3 hours after the first wave, until all the waves have passed and a signal for permission to return is received.

Upon returning, before entering the building, it is necessary to make sure that there is no threat of its collapse due to damage and washing, as well as gas leaks and short circuits in electrical circuits.
